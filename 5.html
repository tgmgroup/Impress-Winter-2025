In most of the world, we say “Happy New Year” on January 1st. But different cultures start their new years on other dates. Let’s learn about some of them!

1 / 9

Lunar New Year

In Asia, January 29, 2025 is the start of the Lunar New Year, also called Chinese New Year. The Lunar New Year starts on the first new moon of the year. This year is the Year of the Snake. People celebrate with dragon dances, fireworks, and special foods.

2 / 9

Nowruz

In Asia and the Middle East, March 20, 2025 is the start of Nowruz, the Persian New Year. People celebrate with special foods, like sabzi polo and ash reshteh, and by jumping over fires.

3 / 9

Songkran

In Thailand, April 13-15, 2025 is the start of Songkran, the Thai New Year. People celebrate by throwing water at each other.

4 / 9

Muharram

For Muslim people, June 25, 2025 is the start of Muharram, the Islamic New Year. People celebrate by fasting and praying. It's not an exciting holiday, but a time to remember the past.

5 / 9

Enkukatash

In Ethiopia, September 11 is the start of Enkukatash, the Ethiopian New Year. People celebrate by giving each other flowers and gifts.

6 / 9

Rosh Hashanah

For Jewish people, September 22-24, 2025 is the start of Rosh Hashanah, the Jewish New Year. People celebrate by eating apples and honey.

7 / 9

Diwali

In India, October 20, 2025 is the start of Diwali, the Festival of Lights. People celebrate by lighting candles and fireworks.

8 / 9

Quviasukvik

For the Inuit people of Alaska and Canada, December 24, 2025 is the start of Quviasukvik. People celebrate by sharing food and gifts with each other.
