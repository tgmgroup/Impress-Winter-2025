Isesaki is not a big city in Japan, but there are quite a few unique things in our town! On October 4th, three of us (David, George, and Nick), went to the Tajima Yahei Sericulture Farm. It’s a World Heritage site and was part of Japan’s silk revolution*. Have you ever been there? We thought we knew the story of this World Heritage, but we took a look* again and learned some new things that we’ll share with you, so keep reading!

* revolution: 革命（かくめい）
* take a look: 見てみる

Keeping Cool:
The Story of Tajima Yahei

Tajima Yahei (1822-1898) changed the way that* silkworms were farmed* in Japan. He understood that it was important to keep silkworms cool. He built a silkworm farm and added a yagura (a roof with windows). This style became known as “Seiryo-iku” and was copied by many Japanese farmers.

* the way that: ～の方法
* to farm: 養殖（ようしょく）
* 農耕（のうこう）する

From Isesaki to Italy

Did you know that Shibusawa Eichi and Tajima Yahei were relatives*? With some help from Mr. Shibusawa, Mr. Tajima started to sell silkworm eggs to Italy and other European countries. In 1879, he visited Italy by ship. It took two months to get there! And he was the first person from Gunma to go all the way* around the world.

* relatives: 親戚（しんせき）
* all the way: 行くところまで・完全に

Under the Microscope

Tajima Yahei learned how to use microscopes* to study silkworms in Europe, and he brought one home from Italy to study sick silkworms. His research helped people grow more silkworms.

* microscope: けんびきょう顕微鏡

Amazing Silk

At this time, the world wanted a lot of silk, and people wanted silk and silkworms from Japan. At its height*, the Tajima Yahei farm employed* around 100 workers and made millions* in today’s* money.

* height: さいせいき最盛期
* employ: やと雇う
* millions: 何百万
* today’s: 現在の
