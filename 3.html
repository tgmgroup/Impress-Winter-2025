The Tradition of Thanksgiving

A long time ago, people came from England to North America. They wanted a new life and new chances*. But it was very, very hard. They needed help from the Native Americans* who lived there. The first Thanksgiving in 1621 was a big meal* to say thank you for the food that helped them live.

* chance: 機会
* Native American: アメリカ先住民
* meal: 食事

1 / 5

Today, Thanksgiving is a time for family to come together* and have a big meal. People have turkey, stuffing, and pumpkin pie. They also watch football and parades on TV. Many people also help others or give food to people in need*.

* come together: 集まる
* people in need: 困っている人々

2 / 5

In Japan, we also have a kind of Thanksgiving Day, too. November 23rd is Labor Thanksgiving Day*. We say thanks to the people who work for us and our society*.

* Labor Thanksgiving Day: 勤労感謝の日
* society: 社会

The Most Challenging All-You-Can-Eats

Japan is not usually known for overeating*, but some Japanese people have made a sport of* eating a lot, such as Takeru Kobayashi and his amazing ability to eat hot dogs. And even if you are not a food athlete, here are some food challenges* that you can try:

* overeating: 食べすぎ
* make a sport of: スポーツにする
* challenge: 挑戦

1 / 6

1. Giant Gyoza Challenge at Kagurazaka Hanten

If you can eat a giant gyoza dumpling of 2.5 kilograms in one hour, it’s free!

 

2 / 6

2. Curry Challenge at Kanazawa Gold Curry

If you can eat 10 kg of curry and rice in one hour, you could win 100,000 yen!

 

3 / 6

3. Ramen Challenge at Umakara Ramen Hyouri

If you can eat 7 kg of ramen in 20 minutes, you can get 50,000 yen!

 

4 / 5

By the way, this is a pie eating contest. You can't use your hands, and the person that eats the most pies wins!

 

5 / 6

These food challenges could be a lot of fun! But on the other hand, maybe it’s better to stick with* regular all-you-can-eat* shabu-shabu, yakiniku, and pizza.

* stick with: ～にこだわる（続く）
* all-you-can-eat: 食べ放題
